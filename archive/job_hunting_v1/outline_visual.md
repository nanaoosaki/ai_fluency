# Outline Visual: Make Yourself Findable

This outline defines the visual scenes for a 6-slide presentation.
**Constraint**: All slides should follow the style direction in `visual_guideline.md` and the style anchor in `imgs/style_matrix_0.jpg`.

**Deck Theme**: Premium, thoughtful, modern, reflective. The talk is not a hype-y career deck. It should feel intelligent, calm, sharp, and slightly cinematic—more like a strategy talk than a motivational seminar.

**Narrative Goal**: This presentation explains a mindset shift in modern job hunting: the market is more information-asymmetric, the system is more broken, and the candidate must become more findable rather than simply apply more.

---

## Slide 1: Why job hunting feels different now
- **Layout**: Structured contrast / 3-part system view.
- **Scene**:
  - **Prompt**: A cinematic, editorial-style opening slide in a premium modern visual language. The overall scene is a calm architectural space made of frosted glass, matte ceramic, and brushed metal. The environment should feel intelligent and minimal, with soft directional light and subtle shadows.
  - Create a central composition that visually communicates three structural changes in the job market.
  - On the left, represent **information asymmetry** as two translucent glass panels facing away from each other, each containing incomplete fragments of data. One panel represents the candidate, the other the market. They do not yet align.
  - In the center, represent a **broken hiring system** as an overloaded filtering mechanism or semi-transparent machine with too many glowing signals flowing through it, causing visual congestion and distortion. It should feel elegant, not dystopian.
  - On the right, represent a **changed work environment** as a shifting architectural corridor with multiple branching paths, suggesting instability, layoffs, role changes, and constant motion.
  - The title should feel like a physical part of the scene, either etched in glass or rendered as floating, refined text: “Why Job Hunting Feels Different Now”.
  - Subtitle or supporting text should read: “Information asymmetry. A broken system. A changed market.”
  - The scene should feel strategic and high-level, not tactical.

## Slide 2: The mindset shift
- **Layout**: Hero statement / conceptual centerpiece.
- **Scene**:
  - **Prompt**: A clean, high-end conceptual slide focused on a single mindset shift. The composition should feel bold but restrained, almost like a cover image for an essay.
  - In the center of the frame, show a poised, elegant visual metaphor of a hunter appearing as prey. Do not depict a literal human hunter. Instead, show a refined luminous form—something like a polished ceramic lure or glowing suspended object—quietly attracting structured signals toward itself. The visual should suggest that the smartest strategy is not chasing frantically, but becoming highly visible and highly relevant.
  - Surrounding the central object, show subtle directional lines or streams of light converging toward it, representing recruiters, roles, and opportunities.
  - Main title rendered prominently: “The Mindset Shift”.
  - Supporting statement rendered clearly but elegantly: “You are not just finding a job. You are making yourself findable.”
  - Optional smaller text near the bottom: “The best hunter appears as prey.”
  - The tone should be calm, strategic, and memorable.

  - imgs/style_matrix_0.jpg

## Slide 3: Make yourself findable
- **Layout**: PMF framework / digital presence environment.
- **Scene**:
  - **Prompt**: A refined spatial scene showing the idea of profile-market fit in a premium digital environment. The setting should feel like a beautifully rendered abstract search interface made physical.
  - At the center, create a strong visual metaphor for **PMF = profile market fit**: a luminous profile card or glass monolith aligning precisely into a search frame or matching slot. The motion should imply that clarity and relevance make the candidate easier to surface.
  - Around the central object, place several supporting objects representing online presence as authentic signals: a translucent LinkedIn-style profile surface, a ceramic portfolio tile, a GitHub-inspired code artifact, and subtle post/comment activity signals hovering like smaller metadata cards.
  - The visual should communicate two things simultaneously: **match** and **authentication**. Match means the profile is legible to the market. Authentication means the person feels real, active, and credible.
  - Render title text: “Make Yourself Findable”.
  - Supporting text can read: “PMF = Profile Market Fit” and “LinkedIn, GitHub, portfolio, posts, comments.”
  - The scene should feel like a modern talent discovery system rendered as architecture.

  - imgs/style_matrix_0.jpg

## Slide 4: What “Data Scientist” means now
- **Layout**: Two-path comparison / future positioning.
- **Scene**:
  - **Prompt**: A premium comparison slide that visualizes the branching future of the Data Scientist role. The scene should be set in a clean architectural environment with two diverging pathways or two elevated platforms.
  - On the left, represent the **Product / Analytics** path. Use visual cues like analytical surfaces, elegant dashboards abstracted into glass layers, experimental loops, and stakeholder-oriented business objects. It should feel interpretive, strategic, and product-facing.
  - On the right, represent the **ML / AI / Engineering** path. Use visual cues like deployment pipelines, modular system components, glowing infrastructure nodes, monitoring signals, and production-grade structure. It should feel technical, operational, and end-to-end.
  - At the center or top, place the title: “What ‘Data Scientist’ Means Now”.
  - Supporting text should make the split explicit: “Product / Analytics” on one side and “ML / AI / Engineering” on the other.
  - Subtle caption can read: “Know your direction before you optimize your signal.”
  - This slide should feel like a map of role evolution, not a generic comparison chart.
- **Asset**:
  - imgs/style_matrix_0.jpg

## Slide 5: Use recruiters well
- **Layout**: Network channel / opportunity flow.
- **Scene**:
  - **Prompt**: A strategic visual scene showing recruiters as a channel into the hidden job market. The environment should suggest flow, access, and selective visibility.
  - Create a composition where opportunities are represented as glowing nodes or chambers in the distance, not all directly visible. Between the candidate and those opportunities stands a network layer of refined gatekeepers or connectors—represented abstractly as elegant glass portals, linked rings, or selective pathways.
  - The key idea is that recruiters are not random intermediaries; they are channels into the opportunity flow. Show one or two paths becoming illuminated when connection is established.
  - Avoid corporate cliché visuals like handshakes or simple icons. This should feel subtle and intelligent.
  - Title: “Use Recruiters Well”.
  - Supporting text can read: “Recruiters are not noise. They are a market access channel.”
  - Optional smaller line: “Sometimes you are not applying to a job. You are entering someone’s radar.”

  - imgs/style_matrix_0.jpg

## Slide 6: Interview = market research
- **Layout**: Reflection / signal collection.
- **Scene**:
  - **Prompt**: A sophisticated final slide showing interview as a two-way information exchange and a process of self-discovery. The visual should feel reflective, composed, and slightly more human than the previous slides without becoming literal.
  - Show a calm central table or reflective glass surface collecting structured light signals from multiple directions. Each incoming beam represents questions, expectations, role signals, or market feedback. On the surface, those signals become clearer, almost like a map being revealed.
  - The composition should communicate that interview is not only evaluation; it is a method of learning the market, refining your story, and understanding your own priorities.
  - In the background, subtly suggest broader life context: location, health, family, remote work, and personal priorities—not as icons, but as faint architectural or environmental layers surrounding the main signal map.
  - Title: “Interview = Market Research”.
  - Supporting text: “The interview process teaches you what the market wants—and what you actually want.”
  - Optional final line near the bottom: “Job hunting is also a process of knowing yourself.”
  - The closing mood should feel thoughtful, grounded, and quietly empowering.
